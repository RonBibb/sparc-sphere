\title{Residual Radial Structure in SPARC Dark Matter Halos Reproduces on
Independently Derived Rotation Curves}

\author[0009-0004-1153-2464]{Ron Bibb}
\affiliation{Independent Researcher, Lilburn, GA, USA}
\email[show]{ronbibb@gmail.com}

\begin{abstract}
Single smooth equilibrium halo profiles (NFW, Burkert, Einasto) leave
statistically significant, radially organized residuals in $\sim$35\% of SPARC
rotation curves: a minimum-order radial-domain decomposition, with model
order selected by the Bayesian Information Criterion, requires a second cored
radial domain in that fraction of galaxies. Such a detection could reflect the
flexibility of the fitting basis or a localized systematic in one velocity-field
extraction rather than a property of the galaxies. We report the most direct test of
galaxy-intrinsic origin: whether the multi-domain selection reproduces when the
rotation curve is re-derived from the original radio data cubes by independent
software, holding the baryonic model fixed so that only the measured velocities
change. It does. In four of five adjudicable multi-domain systems the selection
recovers --- decisively for NGC~2403 ($\dbic = 121$, on a curve derived from
different observations than the SPARC curve) and NGC~3198 ($\dbic = 55$), on a
warp-aware curve for NGC~2841, and at lower significance on a
different-telescope extraction for NGC~3741 --- with the crossover radius
preserved. The one non-reproduction and one power-limited case are reported
explicitly, and single-domain controls reproduce their single-domain selection.
Because a rotation curve constrains only the enclosed-mass profile $M({<}r)$, we make
no claim about the three-dimensional geometry or physical origin of the structure; the
result is confined to a feature in the enclosed-mass profile that is reproducible under
independent velocity derivation. A companion analysis (Bibb 2026b) presents the full method-independence and
robustness program that further characterizes it.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction} \label{sec:intro}

Rotation curves of late-type galaxies are conventionally decomposed into
baryonic contributions plus a single smooth equilibrium dark matter halo ---
cuspy \citep[NFW;][]{Navarro1996}, cored \citep[Burkert;][]{Burkert1995}, or
intermediate \citep[Einasto;][]{Einasto1965}. The adequacy of a single smooth
profile is usually assessed only through global fit quality, and coherent
residual features at particular radii have been discussed largely in connection
with baryonic structure \citep[``Renzo's rule'';][]{Sancisi2004}. Applying a
minimum-order radial-domain decomposition to SPARC rotation curves
\citep{Lelli2016} --- the dark-matter contribution approximated by the smallest number
of cored radial mass components the data support --- we find that a second cored radial
domain is required in $\sim$35\% of galaxies (\S\ref{sec:results}). A companion
analysis (Bibb 2026b) establishes that this selection is robust to the fitting basis
--- stable across NFW, Burkert, and free-shape Einasto backbones, recovered by a
structurally unrelated Gaussian-shell decomposition at consistent radii, and not
predicted by galaxy mass or other measured properties.

Basis-independence, however, does not by itself exclude the possibility that the
structure originates in the specific rotation curves published by SPARC --- for
example in a localized systematic of one velocity-field extraction --- rather
than in the galaxies. This Letter reports the single most stringent test of that
possibility, and the most direct observational test of galaxy-intrinsic origin:
reproduction of the multi-domain selection on rotation curves re-derived from the
original radio data cubes by independent software. A fit improvement on one
published curve, however constrained the basis, does not predict the outcome of
an independent velocity derivation. Such a derivation changes the observable being
fitted, not merely the parameterization: agreement requires the inferred structure to
survive independent measurement, data-reduction choices, and fitting simultaneously.
That this agreement holds is what distinguishes a property of the galaxy from a property
of the parameterization. We state the atomic result --- the
structure survives independent measurement --- and defer the full validation program
to the companion paper.

\section{Data and Methods} \label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Sample, baryonic model, and decomposition}
We use the SPARC database \citep{Lelli2016}, restricted to galaxies with quality
flag $Q \le 2$ and at least six rotation-curve points after removing points
interior to the first reliably measured radius (121 converged fits). Baryonic
contributions adopt fixed mass-to-light ratios $\Upsilon_{\rm disk} = 0.5$ and
$\Upsilon_{\rm bul} = 0.7$ at 3.6~$\mu$m with the gas contribution at unity,
combined in signed squares; velocity uncertainties are floored at 1~km~s$^{-1}$.
The inferred dark-matter contribution is approximated by a minimum-order sum of
cored radial mass components of Burkert form, $V^2_{\rm DM}(r) = \sum_{i=1}^{N}
V^2_{\rm B}(r;\rho_{0,i},a_i)$, with $\rho_{0,i}$ strictly decreasing and $a_i$
strictly increasing outward. Model order is selected by ${\rm BIC} = \chi^2 + k
\ln n_{\rm pts}$ \citep{Schwarz1978,Liddle2007}, $k = 2N$, over $N = 1$--$4$; at
$N = 1$ the model reduces exactly to a single Burkert halo. The capped-fit stability
variant and the full basis-independence program are detailed in the companion paper
(Bibb 2026b); the present Letter applies the same selection procedure and reports its
outcome directly.

\subsection{Independent velocity re-derivation}
The reproduction test holds the baryonic model and the decomposition machinery
fixed and replaces only the measured rotation curve, re-deriving it from the
original radio data cubes by independent software. For NGC~2403 and NGC~3198 we
re-derive curves directly from public THINGS-class cubes using independent
tilted-ring forward-modeling \citep[$^{3\rm D}$Barolo;][]{DiTeodoro2015}, with
geometry fixed to literature tilted-ring solutions. For the strongly warped
systems NGC~2841 and NGC~3521, a blind fixed-geometry extraction cannot test the
feature; we instead use published warp-aware tilted-ring curves (DiskFit;
Sellwood \& Spekkens, private communication) from the same THINGS cubes. For
NGC~3741 we use an independent VLA-ANGST extraction \citep[a different telescope
from the WSRT-based SPARC curve;][]{Walter2008}. Dwarf controls DDO~154 and
DDO~168 use asymmetric-drift-corrected curves from an independent group
\citep[3D forward-modeling;][]{Iorio2017}. Each galaxy's re-derived curve is
passed through the identical selection procedure; a reproduction is a recovery of
the same BIC-selected model order at a consistent crossover radius.

\section{Results} \label{sec:results}

\begin{figure}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figures/fig1_composite.png}
\caption{Representative two-domain decomposition (NGC~2403, SPARC). The observed
rotation curve (points) and baryonic contribution (dotted) are shown with the
best single-Burkert fit (dashed) and the BIC-selected two-domain composite (thick
solid); the inner and outer cored components are shown individually (thin solid).
The single profile cannot reproduce both the inner rise and the outer profile
simultaneously, whereas the two-domain composite does ($\chi^2_\nu$ improving from
$\simeq 19$ to $\simeq 3$). NGC~2403 is the anchor of the present Letter: the same
two-domain selection recovers at $\dbic = 121$ on a rotation curve re-derived from
the original radio cubes by independent software, and from different observations
than those underlying the SPARC curve (\S\ref{sec:results}).
\label{fig:worked}}
\end{figure}

We find that a second radial domain is BIC-selected in $\sim$35\% of the 121 galaxies,
defining the multi-domain population tested here; its full statistical characterization
--- robustness to the fitting basis, decision margins, and the systematic bias a single
smooth fit imposes on the inferred core scale --- is presented in the companion paper
(Bibb 2026b). The present Letter asks the narrower question the reproduction test
answers: when the measured velocities of these galaxies are independently re-derived,
does the same population reappear? Figure~\ref{fig:worked} shows NGC~2403, the anchor
case, in the SPARC data.

The central result of this Letter concerns whether that structure survives an
independent velocity derivation. It does. NGC~2403 selects $N = 2$ at $\dbic =
121$ with the domain crossover at 1.9~kpc (SPARC-based value $\simeq 2.3$~kpc),
on a curve that also derives from different observations than those underlying the
SPARC curve; NGC~3198 selects $N = 2$ at $\dbic = 55$. The warp-aware curve for
NGC~2841 selects $N = 2$ at $\dbic = 11$ --- its earlier failure on a blind
extraction is attributable to the warp suppressing the feature, which the
warp-aware curve recovers. NGC~3741 reproduces at lower significance on the
independent VLA-ANGST extraction, the modest signal-to-noise of this dwarf
limiting the decision margin. The one non-reproduction, NGC~3521, prefers a
single domain on its warp-aware curve ($\dbic = -7$ against two) and is reported
as such; an injection--recovery power control confirms this verdict is
informative rather than power-limited (the feature it would need to show perturbs
the curve well above the detectable amplitude). A single system, NGC~1003, is
genuinely instrument-limited --- its independent HALOGAS extraction yields only 14
usable rings, and a power control shows the feature it would carry lies below the
detectable amplitude --- so its non-reproduction is uninformative. The two
single-domain controls, DDO~154 and DDO~168, reproduce their single-domain
selection.

The adjudicated tally is four reproductions (NGC~2403, NGC~3198, NGC~2841,
NGC~3741) spanning three classes of independence (same cubes/different software,
different observations, and different telescope), one honest non-reproduction
(NGC~3521), one quantified power-limited case (NGC~1003), and two single-domain
controls reproduced. Reproduction holds in four of the five adjudicable galaxies;
the failure and the exclusion are reported explicitly rather than absorbed into an
undifferentiated ``instrument-limited'' category. Because the re-derived
velocities are an independent measurement of each galaxy --- in the strongest
cases from different observations or a different telescope --- a recovery of the
same model order at the same radius is evidence that the structure is consistent with being an intrinsic property of the
galaxy rather than a feature of the particular published velocity extraction.

\section{Discussion} \label{sec:discussion}

We frame this reproduction as proof of principle rather than population-level
validation: the adjudicable sample is small, limited by the subset of SPARC
galaxies whose rotation curves can be re-derived from public cubes at sufficient
quality. It establishes that the multi-domain selection \emph{can} reproduce under
genuinely independent velocity derivation, not the rate at which it does across
the full sample; the latter awaits wider cube availability. What it does establish
is decisive for the interpretation of the phenomenon: the structure is not an
artifact of the specific SPARC velocity extraction, because it survives when that
extraction is replaced.

A boundary on interpretation is geometric and absolute. A rotation curve
constrains only the enclosed-mass profile $M({<}r)$ through $V^2(r) = G
M({<}r)/r$; it carries no information about how the mass producing a given
$M({<}r)$ is arranged in angle or in the third dimension. A second radial domain,
a spherical shell, a planar ring, a stream, a warp-induced redistribution, and a
localized deposit are indistinguishable in $V(r)$ if they enclose the same mass
within each radius. We therefore make no claim about the three-dimensional
geometry of the detected structure, and none about its physical origin --- whether
baryonic \citep[e.g.\ features propagated from the gas disk;][]{Sancisi2004},
kinematic, or a property of the dark matter distribution. The present claim is
confined to what rotation curves can support: a feature in the enclosed-mass profile
reproducible under independent velocity derivation. Discriminating a centrally concentrated from
a localized geometry requires observables that carry angular information ---
azimuthal structure in the velocity field or in resolved tracers --- and is
deferred to analyses of that kind.

The companion paper (Bibb 2026b) presents the full evidence that this structure is
also \emph{method}-independent: its stability across three halo families, its
concordance with an unrelated Gaussian-shell decomposition at consistent radii
(mass-adjusted odds ratio $\sim$37), the forced-fit null that distinguishes
selected structure from basis flexibility, the absorption of the localized shells
by the broad two-domain model, and the negative results that bound the
interpretation. The present Letter isolates the single element of that program
that most directly answers ``is it real'': the structure reproduces on an
independent measurement of the same galaxies.

\section{Conclusions} \label{sec:conclusions}

A minimum-order radial-domain decomposition selects a second radial domain in
$\sim$35\% of SPARC rotation curves. Where the structure is strong enough to test,
that selection reproduces on rotation curves re-derived from the original radio
data cubes by independent software --- in four of five adjudicable galaxies,
decisively for NGC~2403 ($\dbic = 121$) and NGC~3198 ($\dbic = 55$), with the
crossover radius preserved, and with the one non-reproduction and one
power-limited case reported explicitly. The residual radial structure concealed by
single smooth halo fits is therefore consistent with being an intrinsic property of the
galaxies rather than a consequence of a particular published velocity extraction. As to its three-dimensional
geometry and physical origin, the result is deliberately silent: a rotation curve
constrains only the enclosed-mass profile, and those questions require observables
that carry the angular information rotation curves lack.
